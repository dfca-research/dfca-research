Implementing Partner Report

Electricity Subsidy Intervention and Consumption Research, Charsadda

Charsadda District, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan  ·  December 2022 – March 2024

DFCA administered a three-wave panel survey across Charsadda, Tangi, and Shabqadar tehsils in Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, enrolling households connected to the Peshawar Electric Supply Company (PESCO) grid. Enumerator teams completed structured interviews at baseline, midline, and endline with heads of household, collecting electricity bill records, consumption data, and household roster information. In addition to survey administration, DFCA mobilized direct electricity subsidy payments to treated households in Charsadda as part of the intervention design, managing the disbursement of community financial resources across four treatment arms through the study period.

Study Type Randomised controlled trial, three-wave panel survey
Geography Charsadda, Tangi & Shabqadar Tehsils, KP, Pakistan
Survey Waves Baseline (Dec 2022 – Jan 2023), Midline (Sep 2023), Endline (Feb – Mar 2024)

Project Background

Registered study overview.

This study is registered under the American Economic Association's RCT Registry as trial AEARCTR-0011847, with the title "Enhancing Enforcement through Religious Institutions: Experimental Evidence from Pakistan's Power Sector." The study was registered on August 10, 2023, with a planned study period of October 2023 through October 2024.

The research addresses a structural problem in Pakistan's electricity sector: widespread theft and unpaid bills undermine the financial viability of the distribution system and constrain the expansion of electricity access to underserved populations. The distribution network operates as a "leaky bucket" — revenue lost to theft and non-payment prevents the utilities from maintaining infrastructure, subsidising access, and expanding grid coverage.

The central hypothesis is that shifting social norms around electricity payment may represent a cost-effective enforcement mechanism in areas where state capacity is limited. Two treatment arms test this hypothesis: messages delivered by religious leaders during Friday mosque sermons (incorporating Quranic references to the ethics of electricity use), and secular messages delivered by village elders at community gatherings (emphasising practical arguments, such as the direct link between reduced theft and improved electricity supply). A control group receives no intervention. The study measures changes in electricity theft rates, bill collection, and related outcomes across 140 distribution feeders.

DFCA served as implementing partner on the Charsadda field operations, which comprised the household panel survey and subsidy payment mobilisation components of the broader research programme. The Academic Investigators listed below designed the research and are responsible for causal analysis.

Evidence from the Field

Four views of the evidence.

Panel survey completion across three waves

Household counts completed at each survey wave across the three study tehsils. Charsadda Tehsil accounts for the largest share of the sample at each wave, consistent with its higher population density in the study area. Tangi and Shabqadar tehsils each contributed approximately 280–340 households per wave. Overall sample size remained stable across the three waves, indicating low attrition in the panel.

Source: Baseline (n=1,698), Midline (n=1,589), Endline (n=1,631) field records. Charsadda, Tangi, and Shabqadar tehsils, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa.

Observed hooked connection rate by survey wave

Enumerators recorded whether a hooked (illegal) electricity connection was visible at each household at the time of the visit. At baseline (December 2022 – January 2023), 16.0% of households had a visible hooked connection. This rate fell sharply to 2.5% at midline (September 2023), coinciding with the active phase of the intervention. By endline (February – March 2024), the rate returned to 17.9%. These wave-specific observations reflect enumerator assessments at the point of interview and do not constitute a panel estimate of the same households across time.

P1 variable: "Enumerator: Can you spot a hooked connection going directly into the house?" (1=Yes, 2=No, 3=Unsure). Rate shown = P1==1 as share of non-missing responses.

Treatment arm distribution across survey waves

The five study arms — four treatment conditions (T1 through T4) and a control group — were assigned at baseline and tracked across all three survey waves. Each arm contained between 300 and 345 households at baseline. Arm sizes remained comparable across waves, reflecting the panel design and the stability of household participation over the study period. The control group received no subsidy payments during the intervention window.

Source: tr1 variable (baseline/endline), trt variable (midline). Baseline n=1,694; Midline n=1,562; Endline n=1,605 (excluding missing assignments).

Monthly electricity bill distribution at midline

Distribution of reported monthly electricity bills (PKR) recorded from household bill documents at midline (September 2023). The mean bill across all surveyed households was 2,751 PKR, with a median of 2,000 PKR, indicating a right-skewed distribution driven by a subset of high-consumption households. Mean monthly electricity consumption was approximately 160 kWh. Bill amounts were recorded directly from PESCO bills presented by respondents at the time of interview.

IN7 variable: monthly electricity bill amount (PKR). n=1,522 with valid bill records. Midline fieldwork: September 13–23, 2023.

Field observations

What we observed.

The panel maintained high participation rates across all three survey waves.

Of the 1,698 households enrolled at baseline, 1,589 were successfully interviewed at midline (93.6% retention) and 1,631 at endline (96.1%). The geographic distribution of the sample — concentrated in Charsadda Tehsil, with additional clusters in Tangi and Shabqadar — remained consistent across waves, indicating that panel attrition was not systematically concentrated in any single area.

Source: Submission records, Baseline, Midline, and Endline datasets. Charsadda district, 2022–2024.

Monthly electricity bills averaged 2,751 PKR, with significant variation across the sample.

At midline, enumerators collected bill documents from 1,522 households. The mean bill was 2,751 PKR and the median was 2,000 PKR. Mean monthly consumption was approximately 160 kWh. Bills ranged from negligible amounts to over 50,000 PKR, reflecting substantial heterogeneity in household size, appliance ownership, and connection type across the three tehsils. Bill data were verified against PESCO reference numbers recorded by enumerators.

Source: IN7 variable, Midline dataset. Charsadda, Tangi, and Shabqadar, September 2023.

DFCA mobilised subsidy payments to treated households, reaching approximately two-thirds of the eligible midline sample.

At midline, 1,060 of 1,278 households in treatment arms (T1–T4) confirmed receipt of the subsidy payment for the January billing cycle — a delivery rate of approximately 83%. Subsidy amounts were calculated from each household's reported consumption, with payment values in the sample ranging from approximately 300 PKR to over 2,160 PKR per month depending on the assigned treatment rate and units consumed. DFCA administered the disbursement of these funds with full accountability and a high level of integrity.

Source: payment variable (1=received), Midline dataset. xx_feb_pay subsidy values. Charsadda, September 2023.

Treatment arms were well-balanced at baseline and remained so through endline.

Each of the five study arms (T1–T4 and Control) enrolled between 334 and 345 households at baseline. This balance was maintained through midline and endline, with arm sizes remaining within a narrow range of 303 to 325 households per wave. The randomisation produced comparable arm sizes across all three tehsils, supporting the internal validity of the experimental design. The control group, which received no subsidy, comprised approximately 311–340 households across waves.

Source: tr1 and trt variables, Baseline, Midline, and Endline datasets. Charsadda district, 2022–2024.

Visible hooked connection rates varied substantially across the three survey waves.

Enumerator-reported visible hooked connections stood at 16.0% at baseline (271 of 1,698 households) and declined sharply to 2.5% at midline (40 of 1,589 households). By endline, the rate had returned to 17.9% (292 of 1,631 households). These observations reflect what enumerators could identify at the point of interview and do not represent a controlled measurement of behaviour change. Wave-specific differences may reflect seasonal factors, enumerator composition, and the timing of field operations relative to utility enforcement activities.

Source: P1 variable, Baseline, Midline, and Endline datasets. Charsadda district, 2022–2024.

Households in the study area were large, with limited formal employment among non-head members.

The average household size was 6.7 persons at baseline, declining slightly to 5.9 at endline. Working members other than the household head averaged 0.32 at baseline and 0.18 at endline, reflecting limited formal employment in the study area and the economic vulnerability of the population. These characteristics are consistent with a rural, low-income sample in which electricity subsidy payments represent a meaningful share of household income.

Source: b30 (household size) and working_members variables, Baseline and Endline datasets. Charsadda district, 2022–2024.

DFCA administered all three waves of the household panel survey across Charsadda, Tangi, and Shabqadar tehsils, covering 1,698 enrolled households at baseline through to the endline round completed in March 2024. Structured interviews were conducted with the head of household, who was asked to present their current PESCO electricity bill for verification. Enumerators recorded bill reference numbers, monthly units consumed, and total bill amounts directly from the physical bill documents, providing an independent verification layer on self-reported consumption data.

Beyond survey administration, DFCA mobilised electricity subsidy payments to participating households in Charsadda as part of the intervention. This component required DFCA to manage community financial resources — coordinating the calculation of subsidy amounts based on each household's assigned treatment rate and reported electricity consumption, and disbursing payments to households on behalf of the research programme. DFCA executed these financial responsibilities with a high level of integrity, maintaining full accountability for all funds managed throughout the intervention period.

"DFCA mobilised electricity subsidy payments to users in Charsadda, managing community financial resources and executing these responsibilities with a high level of integrity."
