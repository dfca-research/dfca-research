Women's Political Participation and Social Norms in Rural Peshawar

Rural District Peshawar, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan  ·  March–April 2019

DFCA Research conducted a door-to-door household census and social network survey across 37 rural settlements in District Peshawar. Gender-separated field teams administered paired interviews to both female and male household heads in Pashto, completing structured surveys in 3,738 of 4,892 households in the study area. In addition to measuring personal beliefs and community perceptions around women's political participation, field teams collected directed network nominations from every respondent, producing 74 separate social network maps covering female and male ties across all 37 communities.

The Pessimism Wedge

A step-by-step account of pluralistic ignorance.

01 / Personal support is high

More than 82% of women personally agreed that women should discuss politics publicly, have the right to vote, and be able to run for office. Men's personal support is even higher — 93.9% agree women should have the right to vote.

Women · right to vote: 84.5%  ·  Source: Table 1, Panel A, first column.

02 / Women underestimate other women

When women were asked to estimate how many other women in their community would agree with these statements, their estimates fell by 15 to 18 percentage points across all three domains. The gold bars represent actual beliefs; the navy bars show perceived support.

Right to vote gap: 15.5 pp (actual: 84.5%, estimated: 69.0%). Source: Table 1, Panel A.

03 / Men underestimate women even more

Men's estimates of how many women support participation are lower still. The gap between women's actual beliefs and men's estimates of those beliefs reaches 32.8 percentage points for discussing politics. The slate bars show men's estimates.

Discuss politics gap: 32.8 pp (actual: 82.4%, men's estimate: 49.6%). Source: Table 1, Panel A.

04 / Men's own beliefs face the same pattern

Men are personally supportive of women's participation at high rates, but their estimates of how many other men share this view are dramatically lower. The gap reaches 35 percentage points for discussing politics.

Men's personal support (vote): 93.9% vs. 58.5% estimated (−35.4 pp). Source: Table 1, Panel B.

05 / Networks are almost entirely separate

Of the 8.03 average social ties per household, 3.75 are male-only, 3.79 are female-only, and only 0.49 are shared between the male and female head of the same household. Women face a largely distinct social environment.

Total ties: 8.03  ·  Shared ties: 0.49. Source: Table 2, Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

06 / Social neighbors shape beliefs, not husbands

Women's second-order beliefs about community norms show near-zero correlation with the personal beliefs of men in their own household (r = −0.02), while beliefs held by women in their social network show a positive correlation (r = 0.22).

Correlation with household men: r = −0.02. Source: Introduction, Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

Belief trajectory across vantage points — right to vote

Both men and women personally hold high support for women's right to vote. As vantage point shifts from personal belief to estimated community belief, perceived support falls sharply. The gap represents the pessimism wedge documented across 3,738 households.

Underestimation gap by estimating group — averaged across three domains

Each bar shows the average percentage-point gap between first-order beliefs and corresponding second-order estimates. Every group underestimates actual community support. Men's estimates of other men's beliefs carry the largest underestimation (36.4 pp).

Field observations

What we observed.

Personal support for women's political participation was high, but broadly underestimated.

More than 82% of women surveyed personally agreed that women should participate. Women's own estimates of how widely that support was shared among other women averaged 15 to 18 percentage points lower than reality.

Source: Table 1, Panel A. Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

Men's community estimates were the most pessimistic of all groups.

Men personally supported women's right to vote at 93.9%, yet estimated that only 58.5% of other men shared that view. The gap reached 35 percentage points for discussing politics publicly.

Source: Table 1, Panel B. Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

Female and male social networks showed minimal overlap within households.

Households maintained an average of 8.03 social ties. Of these, 3.75 were male-only, 3.79 were female-only, and only 0.49 were shared between heads of the same household.

Source: Table 2. Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

Women's community expectations tracked social neighbors, not husbands.

Women's second-order beliefs showed near-zero correlation with personal beliefs of men in their household (r = −0.02), but positive correlation with women in their own network (r = 0.22).

Source: Introduction. Gulzar, Sonnet and Khan (2025).

DFCA Research conducted a complete door-to-door census across 37 rural settlements in District Peshawar in early 2019. Field teams reached 4,892 households and completed paired male-female interviews in 3,738. Instruments were administered in Pashto by gender-matched enumerators, preserving response independence.

"The segregation of social networks in rural Peshawar drives a profound 'pessimism wedge' where both men and women systematically underestimate the actual levels of support for women's political participation."
