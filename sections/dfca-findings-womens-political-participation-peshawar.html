Norms and Networks: Women's Political Participation in Rural Peshawar

District Peshawar, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan · 2019

We designed and carried out a door-to-door census survey across 37 rural settlements in District Peshawar, collecting primary data on household beliefs, social network ties, and attitudes toward women's political participation. Our field teams administered structured interviews separately to male and female heads of household in each home, using gender-matched enumerators throughout. We also mapped the social networks of every respondent, capturing connections across four relationship types in Pashto, the primary language of the study area. This report documents what our teams collected and observed on the ground.

Study Type Primary Census Survey with Social Network Mapping
Geography Rural District Peshawar, Khyber Pakhtunkhwa, Pakistan
Timeline March to April 2019
Sample Size 3,738 households (both household heads surveyed); 4,892 total households attempted
Survey Rounds Single cross-sectional round

Field Evidence

What we observed on the ground.

Over 80% of women personally supported women's right to vote, discuss politics, and stand for election.

Across all three domains of political participation we measured, personal support among female respondents was high and consistent. Support for the right to vote reached 84.5% of the women we interviewed, the highest rate of the three domains recorded.

Observed across 3,738 households, District Peshawar, 2019

Both men and women substantially underestimated how much support for women's participation existed in their communities.

When asked to estimate how many neighbors held supportive views, respondents of both genders reported figures well below the personal beliefs we recorded. Women underestimated support among other women by 15 to 18 percentage points; men's estimates fell further still across all three domains.

Observed across 37 settlements, District Peshawar, 2019

Women's social networks had almost no overlap with those of men in the same household.

Our network data showed that out of an average of 8.03 total social ties per household, only 0.49 were shared between the male and female head of the same household. Women's ties and men's ties were nearly parallel and separate across all four relationship types we tracked.

Observed across 37 settlements, 3,738 households, 2019

Women more centrally connected in their settlement networks held higher and more accurate expectations about community attitudes.

Among women nominated more frequently by others as a social tie, both personal support levels and estimates of community support were consistently higher. Women with few incoming network ties showed the largest gap between what they believed and what their neighbors actually reported.

Observed across 37 settlements, District Peshawar, 2019

On the Ground

The communities we worked in.

We conducted our fieldwork in rural settlements surrounding Peshawar city, the provincial capital of Khyber Pakhtunkhwa. Our teams travelled door to door across communities that averaged roughly 132 households each and were spread across a district where women's turnout in the 2018 general elections ran nearly 9 percentage points behind men's. Enumerators conducted all surveys in Pashto, the primary language of the area, with verbal consent obtained from every respondent. Women enumerators interviewed female household heads separately and privately inside their homes, allowing frank responses in a context where public expression on these questions is shaped by strong community expectations. Mapping each household's social ties across family, socialization, community discussion, and voting networks added a layer of data rarely collected at this scale in rural KP.

"Voting for women in this setting is a deeply social act. The network ties we mapped shaped what women believed their communities thought — and how willing they were to participate."
